This is for things you want preserved but do not want to actively curate often.

Use this for:

large media dumps,

old collections,

raw recordings,

ZIPs of private repositories,

checksum records,

low-priority artifacts,

superseded snapshots,

material that may be useful for future AI/data mining but is not worth constant attention.

Rule:

Preserve, checksum, label, and stop touching it unless needed.

This fits your M-DISC style well: frozen, read-only, externally verifiable, not endlessly reopened.

The line-drawing problem becomes easier if you stop asking:

“Should I save this or not?”

and instead ask:

“Which layer does this belong in?”

That is the optimization.

A simple decision rule:

SAVE EVERYTHING THAT FEELS NONTRIVIAL.
BUT DO NOT PROMOTE EVERYTHING.
Promotion should be expensive. Capture can be cheap.

For example:

A noisy dictation transcript? Save privately.

A refined principle from that transcript? Promote to canon.

A 2 GB video with unclear value? Cold storage, maybe not public.

A short plain-text note with a useful concept? Private first, maybe canon later.

Original songs or especially significant external songs? Curated audio layer.

Random external songs? Usually no, unless they are unusually important.

Checksums, file sizes, README notes? Keep; those are high-value archival metadata.

Huge binaries? Store deliberately, with checksums, not as casual clutter.

Your M-DISC constraint makes this especially important. Since 25 GB discs are your proven archival unit and usable capacity is roughly in the low-20-GB range, every large MP4/PNG/audio dump has a real physical cost: more discs, more burning, more read-testing, more labeling, more storage complexity. Plain text is almost free by comparison.

So I’d optimize like this:

Capture policy
Be generous.

If it might matter later, save it somewhere.
Canon policy
Be strict.

If it does not improve public KARBYTES, do not canonize it yet.
Media policy
Be selective.

Large files need a reason: original, rare, legally/publicly appropriate, conceptually important, or useful as evidence/context.
Metadata policy
Keep it simple.

Filename, date, source/context, size, checksum, privacy status.
That respects your preference not to overburden the archive with elaborate metadata while still making future retrieval possible.

Review policy
Delay judgment.

Private by default. Review after 6 months. Promote only if it still matters.
This also protects you from mood-based publishing.

The best phrase for your current stance might be:

I am not trying to stop being a data hoarder. I am trying to become a disciplined archivist of my own data hoarding.

Or more technical:

The objective is not deletion. The objective is storage stratification, checksum-backed preservation, and selective promotion.

That seems very compatible with KARBYTES. You can embrace the archival drive without letting it turn the public collection into an undifferentiated pile.
