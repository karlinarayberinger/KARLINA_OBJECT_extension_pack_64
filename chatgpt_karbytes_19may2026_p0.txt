Cone snails: how they paralyze humans

What a cone snail actually does

A cone snail has a modified tooth called a radular tooth. Functionally, it acts like a tiny disposable harpoon or hypodermic needle. The snail extends a proboscis, fires the venom-loaded tooth into prey—or into a human hand if handled defensively—and injects venom. Cone snails use this system to immobilize prey such as worms, mollusks, or fish; the fish-hunting species are the most concerning for humans. 

The most notorious species is the Geography cone. It is often singled out as the most venomous cone snail, with several human deaths attributed to it. 

Why the venom causes paralysis

Cone snail venom is not one simple poison. It is a cocktail of many small peptides, often called conotoxins, that target the nervous system with high precision. Different conotoxins can interfere with ion channels and receptors involved in nerve signaling. In plain terms, they can disrupt the electrical and chemical messages that tell muscles to contract. 

That can produce:

tingling or numbness near the sting site

spreading numbness or weakness

muscle paralysis

vision changes in severe cases

respiratory failure if the muscles needed for breathing become paralyzed

Severe cases are dangerous mainly because breathing can fail. The venom does not need to “destroy” the lungs; it can stop the nerve-muscle signaling needed to breathe. 

Is cone snail paralysis permanent or temporary?

Usually, the paralysis is temporary if the person survives and receives supportive care, because the main emergency is keeping the person alive while the toxins wear off. National Geographic describes treatment as limited to keeping victims alive until the toxins wear off, because there is no antivenom. 

So the basic answer is:

Temporary paralysis: most characteristic of cone snail envenomation if treated successfully.

Potentially fatal paralysis: if breathing muscles are affected and ventilation/support is not available.

Permanent damage: not usually the defining feature, but complications can happen indirectly—especially from prolonged oxygen deprivation, drowning, trauma from the incident, or delayed emergency care.

Why there is no simple antidote

Cone snail venom varies by species and contains many different toxins. That complexity makes a single universal antivenom difficult. Medical care is therefore mainly supportive: pressure immobilization may be used in some first-aid guidance, and emergency treatment focuses on breathing support, monitoring, pain control, and preventing complications. 

Practical rule

If you ever see a beautiful cone-shaped shell in tropical marine water: do not pick it up if the animal may still be inside. The shell is the lure for human curiosity; the living snail is the hazard.
